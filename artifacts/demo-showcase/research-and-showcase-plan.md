# Research and showcase plan

Date: 5 July 2026

## Research findings

1. **Lead with a functioning product moment.** Stripe Atlas says early-stage
   pitches most often fail because they are unclear about what is actually
   being built, and recommends having a demo ready to show in 20 seconds.
   Source: [Stripe Atlas — Pitching your early-stage startup](https://stripe.com/en-ca/guides/atlas/pitching).
2. **Use a short teaser and a separate walkthrough.** Wistia's analysis of more
   than 100 million videos recommends less than one minute for awareness, while
   noting that engagement falls as length increases. Loom describes a two- to
   three-minute introductory demo as the format for deciding whether to learn
   more. Sources: [Wistia — optimal video length](https://wistia.com/learn/marketing/optimal-video-length),
   [Loom — product demonstration videos](https://www.loom.com/blog/product-demonstration).
3. **Make the customer problem the story, not a feature inventory.** Loom
   recommends audience-specific problem/solution storytelling, a storyboard,
   and a clear call to action. Stripe's guide to first customers emphasizes
   founder authenticity, understanding the problem, and learning one customer
   at a time rather than pretending to have repeatable demand. Sources:
   [Loom — product demonstration videos](https://www.loom.com/blog/product-demonstration),
   [Stripe Atlas — your first 10 customers](https://stripe.com/guides/atlas/starting-sales).
4. **Trust mechanics belong in the main story for legal workflows.** In the
   Thomson Reuters 2025 professional-services survey, accuracy and
   misinformation remained central concerns; the report frames these systems
   as augmentation that still requires professional scrutiny. Source:
   [Thomson Reuters — 2025 GenAI report executive summary](https://legal.thomsonreuters.com/blog/genai-report-executive-summary-for-legal-professionals-tri/).
5. **Caption the video and narrate visual changes.** W3C guidance requires
   captions for prerecorded audio and recommends integrating descriptions of
   important visual information into planned narration. Sources:
   [W3C — captions for prerecorded media](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/Understanding/captions-prerecorded),
   [W3C — description of visual information](https://www.w3.org/WAI/media/av/description/).

These sources inform presentation technique only. They do not validate demand
for F-001.

## Recommended showcase system

Create both:

- a **35–45 second teaser** for the first share or message; and
- a **90–120 second walkthrough** for a trusted friend or prospective design
  partner who opts in to more detail.

The first 20 seconds of each version should show the actual decision surface:
three documents, three superficially plausible numbers, one current
contractual position.

## Clearest problem framing

> An original agreement says 65%. A later amendment says 70%. A waiver mentions
> 72%. Which number can a reviewer actually rely on today—and what else does the
> waiver restrict?

This is narrower and more credible than “AI for agreements.” It names the
reviewer's concrete risk: applying a limited waiver as though it permanently
amended the covenant.

## Strongest before-versus-after moment

**Before:** the reviewer sees 65%, 70%, and 72% across three documents and must
manually reconstruct scope and dates.

**After:** the dashboard says “Amendment controls; waiver remains limited,”
shows the 70.0% threshold, isolates the 72.0% Test Date relief, flags the
separate no-Distribution condition, and links to the exact supporting passage.

## What to narrate

- The reviewer's decision and the cost of using the wrong source.
- Why 72.0% is relief, not the amended covenant.
- Why the separate distribution restriction changes the next action.
- Why the system abstains or requests human review when support is incomplete.

## What to show

- Current dashboard status and the three changes.
- Date switching from original to amendment/waiver to expiry.
- Timeline with the three document roles kept distinct.
- The exact waiver passage opened from the recommendation.
- One supported answer, one `Source not found.` answer, and one
  legal-review-required answer.

## What to omit

- Architecture diagrams, schema details, database adapter terminology, and
  hosted-product plans.
- The full navigation tour in the main video.
- Claims about market demand, time saved, accuracy rates, or reviewer reactions
  that have not been observed.
- A feature checklist or exaggerated “replace legal review” language.

## Trust details that must remain visible

- Synthetic-only label.
- As-of date.
- Original agreement, amendment, and waiver shown as separate events.
- Document title, locator, page, and exact passage.
- Deterministic/local boundary.
- Explicit abstention and human-review states.
- Closing statement that legal interpretation and commercial judgment remain
  human responsibilities.

## Tone and call to action

Tone: calm, specific, and slightly tense at the opening; relieved but not
triumphal at the resolution.

Call to action:

> Would this make your first-pass review clearer? What would you still need to
> verify before relying on it?

That question is appropriate for a trusted friend and later design partners. It
seeks workflow evidence without manufacturing endorsement.
